Business Haiku

When you get clouded in the details, come back to the basics of business:

Company -> Customer

Customer -> Company

In terms of the Business Model Canvas:

Company -> Customer = Key Resources -> Customer Segments

Customer -> Company = Customer Segments -> Key Activities

Tip for figuring out monetization

  • If you have no other intuition from previous experience, consider yourself.

    • (Assuming you are in the target audience) Would you buy your product for a price that would satisfy your production costs?

  • Consider your audiences and their gaming/money habits.

    • eg. In F2P: High money low time VS Low money high time

Types of Game Studios

  • Work for Hire: Contract service. Customer is the Publisher.
  • Games as a Product: One-time purchase, DLC, Trials, P2P.
  • Games as a Service: F2P, Subscription-based, P2P, Ad-supported.

Customer Acquisition

The value of one customer should be more than the cost to acquire them.

Brenda Gershkovitch Fireside Chat

  • Gaming business is changing.
    • Try to figure out the future instead of looking at the past.
    • Try to make new and exciting games. ("Do we really need another baseball game?" - This comes from her experience working on AAA franchises.)
  • Working with publishers will be amazing sometimes and dreadful other times.
    • Self-publishing might be less profitable, but Brenda says it is more fun.
  • "Women are 50% of gamers"
    • But this is mostly in casual. eg. 75% female on Facebook.
    • Still mostly men on PS4, Steam, etc.
    • Brenda hoping that casual space women will translate into "core" gamers.
  • Core vs Casual: Defined by gaming culture for gaming culture. Not necessarily useful for business descriptions.
  • Steven Spielberg: "The next big thing in gaming is romance."
    • This is currently exactly Sillicon Sisters business.
    • They are connecting on Amazon with people who buy romance novels. (Their games show up in related suggested products.)
    • This allows reaching brand new audiences.
  • Figure out where people are not building. Figure out where customers are starved.
    • eg. The few initial games on Kindle made bucketloads of money.
    • Brenda: "Don't make games you want to play. Make games they want to play."
    • Brenda: "[if] we keep making games for ourselves, the industry is not going to change."
  • Sillicon Sisters: Why are girls not playing games? (Their business is girl games.)
    • Most people think: "girls do not play games."
    • Thus, little investment in girl games. Thus, most girl games are shit.
    • Motivation: Brenda wanted games to exist for her daughter.
    • SS interviewed tween girls.
      • Some like the same games as boys (killing aliens, etc.)
      • Girls like social games. (eg. Gossip, social intelligence.)
      • In their experience: Make character that girl gamers sympathize with. (Was not easy. Testing was important.)
  • Some studios Brenda has been in felt like a frat house.
    • Wild West is fun and creative, and frat might be fun.
    • Still, keep it professional and welcoming.
    • "Frat house" alienates talented people who will make you lots of money.
    • Brenda: Cleaning up the "frat house" studio and bringing women in was very well received.
      • People had lots of fun, got girlfriends, etc.

Daniel Jacobsen Fireside Chat

  • Gaslamp: Daniel + Nicholas + David
  • Started tactial RPG. Found scope to be too large (too many assets.)
    • They had 5 people on the team. People left due to no confidence.
  • Gave up on tactical RPG, unshelved Dredmor prototype.
  • Dungeons of Dredmor was not originally fun.
    • The whole game was remade many times. Lots of design trial and error.
    • Originally supposed to be innovative roguelike.
    • Innovative design sucked. Tried traditional design.
    • Traditional design boring. Tried quirky style.
    • Drew inspiration from board games for combat. Was still missing game elements.
  • After working on Dredmor for 3 years, they found 6 months before launch that it was viable.
  • They had to get friends to playtest because they could not judge the game (they were desensitized.)
  • They started a development blog to get feedback from people.
  • Got a few readers from people looking for roguelikes (in the niche.)
    • 10ish people were reading their blog. They wondered who they were.
    • Found threads on roguelike forums talking about Dredmor and interacted with them. Answered their questions, got them engaged.
    • Lots of success was found in small communities.
    • A thread on SomethingAwful built a lot of community.
  • This community of ~1000 people bought the game instantly on Steam, so Dredmor rose to the top sellers instantly.
  • Famous Youtube reviewers found the game on the top sellers list, reviewed them.
  • Steam is now less exclusive. Dredmor got on Steam because they "knew a guy who knew a guy".
  • Try not to just show up on forums to advertise.
    • Get discussions going, then find and join the discussions.
    • Develop community around your studio. ("Gaslamp Games players for life")
  • Communicate with platform (talk to Kongregate, Steam...)
    • Dan phones Valve, asks what they want and help them out. (eg. Using new features of Steam.)
    • Keep customer interests first, but also value "gatekeeper" interests.
  • Bringing a prototype at industry events (eg. GDC):
    • Either have a very complete prototype (attractive because low risk.)
    • Or have lots of experience so they can have confidence in you.

Positioning Statement

For $(target customer) who $(statement of need or opportunity), the $(product name) is a $(product category) that $(compelling reason to buy/try). Unlike $(competition), our product $(statement of primary differentiation).

Channels

  • Consider statistics on what genres do the best on a given channel. (What are the strongest genres? Weakest genres?)
  • Beware of channel promises vs reality.
    • As a business, they are trying to sell their channel.
    • Maintain your own statistics in parallel with theirs to confirm beliefs.
  • In a saturated market, need lots of money to fund the awareness.

User Acquisition

  • Cost of acquiring a user should be less than how much money they give you over their lifetime.
  • Want to optimize the user acquisition cost.
  • Reduce acquisition costs by finding an opportunity that works naturally.
    • Innovative design, underserved platforms, licensed IP, niche genres.
  • "Why would someone promote your game?"

Interacting with Customers

You have worked to get players/customers, now how will you interact with them?

Know top spenders, focus on them. Identify ways to focus on them.
